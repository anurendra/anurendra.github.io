Bio

Postdoctoral Researcher, Stanford University: computational genomics, causal inference, and AI for drug discovery.

From association to mechanism: spatial genomics and perturbation data for drug prioritization.
Many omics analyses are shaped by confounding structure, which can produce convincing but non-reproducible discoveries. My research develops statistical and machine learning frameworks that account for tissue architecture, local composition, and spatial context, so that the signals that remain are more likely to reflect biology rather than artifact.

My current work connects spatial transcriptomics and perturbation atlases to identify mechanistically grounded therapeutic targets in complex disease, with particular interest in immune evasion in cancer and genetic cardiac disease, including DCM. A growing focus is transportable perturbation modeling: understanding what is conserved versus context-specific in intervention responses, and how signals from large cell-line atlases can be responsibly transferred to disease-relevant systems such as iPSC-derived models. The through-line across this work is causality: without it, even rich spatial or perturbational data often produces prioritization lists that do not translate.

My PhD work on subcellular RNA organization (InSTAnT, Nature Communications 2024) and cell–cell communication in spatial data (CellWHISPER, bioRxiv 2026) was driven by the same insight: structured confounding is a dominant failure mode in spatial biology, and conditioning on it can change biological conclusions.

Before my PhD, I interned at Genentech, Vertex, IBM Research (4×), and others, and briefly attempted a startup, which failed productively.
