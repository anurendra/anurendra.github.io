Bio

Postdoctoral Researcher, Stanford Medicine (Wu Lab) — computational genomics & AI for drug discovery.

From data to discovery: spatial genomics + perturbation for mechanism and drug prioritization.
I’m interested in how gene programs and cell–cell interactions shape tissue state in health and disease—and how we can leverage that understanding to prioritize therapeutic interventions. My research sits at the intersection of statistical modeling, machine learning, and single-cell/spatial genomics.

Methodologically, I build scalable, interpretable models for high-dimensional omics data: latent variable models, representation learning, and confounder-aware statistical tests. I care a lot about rigor—stress effects, cell-type composition, spatial density, and other confounders can easily produce convincing but wrong signals, so my work is designed to be robust to these failure modes.

My recent work focuses on (i) subcellular spatial transcriptomics (how RNAs organize within cells) and (ii) direct cell–cell communication in tissues (including contact-mediated signaling and gap junction biology). In parallel, I’m increasingly excited about perturbation transcriptomics (LINCS/Tahoe/Parse) as a route to connect mechanisms to actionable drugs. A key piece of my current direction is transportable perturbation modeling: learning what is conserved versus context-specific in intervention responses, so signals measured in large cell-line atlases can be responsibly transferred to disease-relevant systems (e.g., iPSC-derived models) with uncertainty estimates.

Before my PhD, I worked on diverse ML problems (audio source separation, VAEs, and graph attention models for information extraction). I’ve also interned at Genentech, Vertex, IBM Research (4×), ServiceNow, Rivigo, and Samsung. After graduating, I tried a startup project—an experiment that failed, but taught me a lot about building end-to-end systems and staying resilient when things don’t work the first time.
